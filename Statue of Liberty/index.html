Statue of Liberty

The Statue of Liberty, a gift from France to the United States in 1886, symbolizes freedom and democracy. Designed by Frédéric Auguste Bartholdi and engineered by Gustave Eiffel, it stands on Liberty Island in New York Harbor. The statue, made of copper and standing 151 feet tall, represents Libertas, the Roman goddess of liberty, holding a torch in one hand and a tablet inscribed with the date of American independence in the other.

Statue of Liberty

The statue's construction was a remarkable engineering feat, with its internal framework designed by Eiffel, ensuring stability and strength. Over the years, the Statue of Liberty has welcomed millions of immigrants arriving by sea, becoming a beacon of hope and opportunity. Its inscription, "Give me your tired, your poor, your huddled masses yearning to breathe free," reflects its enduring message of freedom and refuge.

In 1984, the Statue of Liberty was designated a UNESCO World Heritage Site, recognizing its cultural and historical significance. Today, it remains one of the most visited monuments in the world, attracting millions of tourists each year. The statue continues to stand as a powerful symbol of America's ideals and the enduring friendship between France and the United States.
